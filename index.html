Associate Professor · George Mason University

How does kinship structure shape psychology, institutions, and economic development — and what can we do about it?

I study cultural evolution: the co-evolution of human psychology with formal and informal institutions, and how deep social structures — particularly kinship networks — shape the contemporary world. My research combines historical data, natural experiments, and cross-cultural methods to answer questions at the intersection of economics, psychology, and political science.

I am Co-PI of the Historical Psychology Project at Harvard, a Research Fellow at the Mercatus Center, and serve on the board of ASREC. In 2023 I received the Royal Economic Society Prize.

The Church, Intensive Kinship, and Global Psychological Variation

With D. Bahrami-Rad, J. Beauchamp, and J. Henrich · Science, 2019, 366(6466)

data media
We propose that the Western Church (the Roman Catholic Church before the Great Schism of 1054) transformed European kinship structures beginning in the Early Middle Ages. Specifically, the Church's Marriage and Family Program (MFP), which proscribed kin-based marriages and mandated nuclear family structures, disrupted the intensive kinship networks that had characterized most human societies throughout history. Using observational data across countries, regions, and individuals, we find that longer exposure to the Western Church (and MFP) is strongly associated with less intensive kinship (e.g. lower rates of cousin marriage), and with more individualism, less conformity, and more analyticity.

Psychological Change and Kinship Intensity in China over Two Millennia

With Yuqi Chen, Mohammad Atari, Edward Slingerland, Ze Hong, Xiaokang Fu, Hongsu Wang, Peter Bol, and Joseph Henrich

Working paper Preprint
Using two classical Chinese corpora, we apply a computational text-analysis pipeline to capture psychological characteristics across time (770 BCE to 1911 CE) and space (270 prefectures). Our results show substantial regional variation and non-linear temporal dynamics, and find support for the hypothesis that family organizations — captured by kinship intensity — predictably co-evolve with particular socio-cooperative aspects of psychology.

Kin-based Institutions and Economic Development

With Duman Bahrami-Rad, Jonathan Beauchamp, and Joseph Henrich · Review of Economic Studies, Revise & Resubmit

Kin-based institutions are the set of social norms governing descent, marriage, and post-marital residence. We establish a robust and economically significant negative association between the tightness and breadth of kin-based institutions — their kinship intensity — and economic development. To measure kinship intensity and economic development, we deploy genotypic measures and quantified ethnographic observations on kinship with data on satellite nighttime luminosity and regional GDP. Our results are robust to controlling for a suite of geographic and cultural variables and hold across countries, within countries at both the regional and ethnolinguistic levels, and within countries in a spatial regression discontinuity analysis.
